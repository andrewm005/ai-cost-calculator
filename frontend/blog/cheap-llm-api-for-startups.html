Cheapest LLM APIs for Startups in 2026

Early-stage products have an unusual budget situation: the bill is small in absolute terms but the per-user cost is a real fraction of revenue. Picking the right cheap model matters. So does knowing when to graduate to the flagship. Together they're the difference between a 70% gross margin and a thin one. the cheapest paid models on OpenRouter, the free tier for prototyping, and the specific workload shapes where the cheap models actually work.

The cheap end of the catalog

The cheapest paid models on OpenRouter as of mid-2026 are clustered in the open-weights category. Llama 3.1 8B Instruct from Meta is $0.02 per 1M input tokens and $0.03 per 1M output tokens. Phi-4 from Microsoft is $0.07/$0.14. Llama 3.3 70B at $0.10/$0.32 is the cheapest 70B-class model. Mistral Small 3.1 24B at $0.35/$0.56 is the cheapest non-Meta option in the mid-tier band.

The per-call cost on these models is essentially free at low volume. A chat-shaped call (1,000 in + 500 out) on Llama 3.1 8B is $0.000035. At 1 million calls per month, that's $35. The same call on GPT-4o is $0.0075, which is $7,500 per month. The cheap models buy a 200x cost reduction at the cost of some quality on hard tasks.

The free tier

OpenRouter lists 26 models at $0 input and $0 output. The list includes Llama 3.2 3B Instruct, several Gemma 4 variants, Liquid's LFM 2.5 1.2B, and a handful of community-finetuned open-weights models. They are tagged with a `:free` suffix in the slug.

Free models are prototyping tools, not production tools. The rate limits are tighter than the paid tier. Typically only a few requests per minute rather than a few hundred. Latency varies depending on the lab's GPU availability. The labs reserve the right to retire the model with little notice. A startup that launches on a free model and grows into real traffic needs a migration plan to a paid tier within a quarter or two.

The legitimate use of the free tier is evaluation. If you're picking between Llama 3.1 8B and Mistral Small for a real workload, you can run a few thousand free requests against Llama 3.2 3B and Gemma 4 31B to see whether the family is competitive for your use case. The free tier is also the right place for hackathon projects, demos, and any non-production traffic.

What the cheap models are good at

The cheap models are good at the same tasks the flagship models are good at, with a quality penalty on the hard end of the distribution. Specifically:

Classification. Sentiment analysis, topic labeling, intent detection, and any task where the output is one of N predefined categories. Llama 3.1 8B and Phi-4 are both competitive with the flagships on standard classification benchmarks. The cost difference is irrelevant to the user experience.

Extraction. Pulling structured data out of unstructured text such as names, dates, amounts, and addresses. The cheap models are good enough that the flagship isn't worth the premium for most extraction workloads.

Short chat. Customer support replies, FAQ bots, and short conversational turns. The cheap models handle these at acceptable quality. The flagship's edge on long, nuanced, multi-turn conversation is irrelevant when the average reply is 80 tokens.

Translation and short summarization. Both tasks are well-served by small models trained on multilingual and summarization data. Phi-4 and Llama 3.1 8B are competitive here.

What the cheap models are not good at

Long-form synthesis. Tasks that require the model to read a long document and produce a coherent summary or analysis. The 8B and 24B parameter models have weaker long-context reasoning than the flagships, and they lack the context window, and Llama 3.1 8B tops out at 131K tokens but its quality degrades well before that.

Complex reasoning. Math word problems, multi-step logic, and any task where the model needs to think before answering. The flagships win by a meaningful margin here. For reasoning workloads, DeepSeek R1 at $0.70/$2.50 is the cost-conscious pick. Roughly 1/24th the cost of OpenAI o1 at comparable quality on most problems. See the reasoning token article for the full breakdown.

Reliable tool use. Agents that call tools ten times in a single task need the model to call each tool correctly on the first try. The flagships (Claude Sonnet 4, GPT-4o) hit 90%+ first-try accuracy on most tool-use benchmarks. The cheap models hit 70-85%. The difference is the difference between a single agent loop and a multi-loop agent with extra API calls per task, which multiplies the per-task cost by 2-3x.

The decision rule

Start on the cheapest model that does the work. As of mid-2026 that is Llama 3.1 8B at $0.02/$0.03. Move up the tier ladder when customers notice quality problems on tasks the flagship handles better. Three signs it's time to move up:

First, customer complaints about answer quality on specific task types. Second, the workload needs long-context windows the cheap model can't handle. Third, the agent's tool-call accuracy on the cheap model is below 90% on the workloads that matter.

The migration path is mechanical: route the failing workload to the flagship, keep the easy workloads on the cheap model. Most production systems end up running 3-4 models. The cheap one for easy traffic, the flagship for the hard 10-20%, and sometimes a reasoning model for the hardest 1-2%.

The bill at scale

The headline rate is the input; the bill is the output. A startup running 5 million chat-shaped calls per month on Llama 3.1 8B at $0.02/$0.03 per 1M tokens is paying roughly $175/month in API costs. The same traffic on GPT-4o is $37,500/month. The same traffic on GPT-4o mini is $2,250/month. The cheap model buys two years of runway at the same traffic level, which is the actual point of starting on the cheap tier.

The trap is staying on the cheap tier when customers notice quality problems. A support chatbot on Llama 3.1 8B that misroutes 15% of customer questions is more expensive than a chatbot on the flagship that misroutes 2%, even after the per-call cost difference. The cheap tier is the right choice when the model's quality is good enough for the workload; it becomes the wrong choice the moment it isn't.

The ramp-up rule

Start with the cheapest model that clears your quality bar and only upgrade when the failure mode is something the cheaper model genuinely cannot handle. Switching tiers mid quarter is cheap; re-architecting around a model you outgrew in week one is expensive. Re-run the cost math every time you change a tier, the rate cards move faster than most teams update their estimates.

Frequently asked questions

What's the cheapest paid LLM on OpenRouter?

Llama 3.1 8B Instruct from Meta is the cheapest paid production-quality model at $0.02 per 1M input tokens and $0.03 per 1M output tokens. Phi-4 from Microsoft is next at $0.07/$0.14. Both are good at short chat, classification, and extraction.

Are free models on OpenRouter safe for production?

Free models (26 as of mid-2026) are best treated as prototyping tools. They have tighter rate limits, variable latency, and the labs can take them offline with little notice. They are great for evaluating a model family; they are not reliable enough for production traffic.

Is Mistral Small better than Llama 3.1 8B?

For most short-form workloads, no. Llama 3.1 8B at $0.02/$0.03 is meaningfully cheaper than Mistral Small 3.1 24B at $0.351/$0.555. Mistral Small wins on multilingual support and a few instruction-following benchmarks, but for plain English chat, classification, and extraction, Llama 3.1 8B is the cheaper default.

When is a startup ready to move off the cheap models?

When the flagship's quality matters more than the bill. Three signs: customers complain about answer quality, the workload needs long-context windows the cheap models can't support, or the agent's tool-call accuracy is below 90% on the cheap model.
