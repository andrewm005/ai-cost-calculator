Every Major LLM in One Cost Table (2026)

The 1,300x price gap between Llama 3.1 8B and Claude Opus 4 is real, and it reflects a 1,000x difference in serving cost per token. Sorting 23 models by chat-shaped call cost is the fastest way to see where the cheap tier ends and the flagship tier begins. Twenty-three models, one table, sorted by total cost for a typical chat call. The numbers below come from the OpenRouter public feed, refreshed every six hours on AI Cost Calculator. Flagship models from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, DeepSeek, Mistral, xAI, Cohere, and Qwen sit side-by-side, with the cheap, mid-tier, and premium tiers shown for each provider where they exist.

How to read the table

The "Input $/M" and "Output $/M" columns are the per-million-token rates as published by the upstream vendor and surfaced through OpenRouter. The "Cache $/M" column is the cached input rate where the vendor publishes one. The "1k+500" column is what a single chat-shaped call costs: 1,000 input tokens at the input rate, 500 output tokens at the output rate. The "10k+2k" column is a longer-form workload: 10,000 input and 2,000 output, closer to a RAG or coding workload.

Sorted by the typical-call column, cheapest first. Reasoning tokens, tool call surcharges, and batch discounts are excluded. See the reasoning token article and the cost reduction article for those multipliers. All numbers are current as of the publish date; refresh the page or check each model's page for the live rate.

What the table tells you

The cheap end of the table is dominated by open-weights models: Llama 3.1 8B at $0.00004 per chat-shaped call is roughly 1,300x cheaper than Claude Opus 4 at $0.05250 for the same shape. The gap reflects real capability differences on hard tasks, but on classification, extraction, and short chat, the cheap models close the gap with the flagships at a fraction of the price. The structural reason is compute: an 8B model runs on a single consumer GPU; a flagship 70B+ model needs a rack of high-end GPUs. The inference cost is dominated by GPU-hours, and the cheap tier passes the savings through to the per-token rate.

The flagship tier clusters around $2.00-$3.00 input and $8.00-$15.00 output. GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Cohere Command A, and Grok 4.20 all sit in this band, with differences in cache rates and context window that matter more than the headline rate for most workloads. The GPT-4o vs Claude Sonnet 4 comparison walks through the side-by-side math for one of these flagship pairs.

The reasoning tier is its own band. DeepSeek R1 at $0.70/$2.50 is the cost-conscious pick. Chain-of-thought reasoning at roughly 1/24th the cost of OpenAI o1. OpenAI's o3 at $2.00/$8.00 and o4-mini at $1.10/$4.40 are mid-tier reasoning options. o1 and Claude Opus 4 are the premium reasoning tier, suitable for problems where reasoning quality is worth paying for. See the reasoning token article for the full breakdown of how reasoning affects the bill.

Reading across rows

A useful exercise is to compare the cheap and flagship entries from the same provider. Anthropic's Claude 3 Haiku at $0.25/$1.25 is the budget tier, Claude Sonnet 4 at $3.00/$15.00 is the flagship, and Claude Opus 4 at $15.00/$75.00 is the premium tier. The same shape is true on OpenAI. GPT-4o mini at $0.15/$0.60, GPT-4o at $2.50/$10.00, and o1 at $15.00/$60.00. Google's Gemini 2.5 family runs from Flash at $0.30/$2.50 to Pro at $1.25/$10.00 with no published Gemini Ultra tier on the platform at the moment. When a workload needs to scale across many calls, the budget tier is the right default; the flagship tier is the right call when the answer quality justifies the price.

A second useful exercise is to compare flagship-to-flagship across providers. GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Cohere Command A, and Grok 4.20 all sit within a 3x range of each other on output rates. The differentiators are not the headline rates; they are the cache rate (Anthropic and OpenAI publish aggressive cache discounts, Gemini has implicit caching), the context window (Gemini 2.5 Pro and Claude Sonnet 4 reach 1M tokens, GPT-4o tops out at 128K), and the quality on the specific task the application needs. The GPT-4o vs Claude Sonnet 4 comparison walks through how those differentiators show up on a real workload. Clearly, flagship pricing is clustered, but the multipliers are where the bill actually moves.

Where the cheap models stay cheap

Llama 3.1 8B Instruct at $0.02/$0.03 is roughly 1,300x cheaper per chat-shaped call than Claude Opus 4. The reason is structural. A 70B-parameter model requires multiple high-end GPUs to serve at reasonable latency; an 8B model runs on a single consumer GPU. The inference cost is dominated by the GPU-hours, not the number of tokens, and an 8B model uses about 1/10th the GPU-time per token of a 70B model. The labs that serve the cheap models pass the GPU-cost savings through to the per-token rate.

The catch is capability. Llama 3.1 8B handles classification, extraction, and short replies at a level that closes the gap with the flagships. It does not handle long-form synthesis, complex reasoning, or any task where the flagship's larger context window and stronger training show up in the output. For workloads the cheap model can do, the per-call cost is roughly free at scale. When the flagship is the right answer, no right answer, no amount of cost optimization closes the gap to the cheap tier. The choice is structural — use the model that does the work.

What the table doesn't show

The table shows the headline rate, but deployed bills are shaped by the multipliers covered elsewhere on this site. Prompt caching cuts input by 50-90% depending on provider. System prompts ship on every call and can be a fixed cost worth thousands of dollars a month at scale. Reasoning tokens multiply the per-call cost by 2-10x depending on the model and the question. Tool calls grow the conversation history and re-bill it on every turn.

The right way to read the table is as the input to a real estimate, not the estimate itself. Plug in the actual token counts, account for the multipliers, and the per-call cost moves up or down. The calculator handles the multipliers; the model pages linked above are the source of truth for the rates.

Frequently asked questions

What is the cheapest flagship-tier LLM in 2026?

Among the major flagship models, Gemini 2.5 Pro is the cheapest on input at $1.25 per 1M tokens. Mistral Small 3.1 24B at $0.351 input / $0.555 output is the cheapest mid-tier model. Llama 3.1 8B at $0.02 input / $0.03 output is the cheapest production-quality model on the platform.

What is the most expensive LLM in 2026?

Among the publicly listed models, Claude Opus 4 at $15.00 input and $75.00 output per 1M tokens is the most expensive flagship on the platform. OpenAI's o1 is $15.00 / $60.00. Some specialty models push past $100 per 1M output.

Do all models on the platform have the same pricing structure?

Most publish input, output, cached input, and a tool_call_cost field. Some publish a reasoning_per_1m field for chain-of-thought billing. The free models publish $0 across the board. The structure is consistent; the values are not.

How often does the platform refresh pricing?

AI Cost Calculator refreshes its OpenRouter cache every six hours. The OpenRouter feed itself can update more or less frequently depending on vendor changes.
