About GPT-4.1 Nano

GPT-4.1 Nano is a budget-tier model on OpenRouter, priced at $0.10 per 1M input tokens and $0.40 per 1M output tokens. It is the right pick for high-volume work where the cost per call matters more than the absolute best answer quality: bulk classification, extraction pipelines, simple chat agents, and the kind of background work that fires thousands of times a day. It is also a sensible default for prototypes where the team has not yet decided which model they want to standardize on, since the bill will be small even if the volume is high.

At $0.10 per 1M input tokens and $0.40 per 1M output tokens, the cost of a single call is dominated by output unless the workload is very output-heavy or very input-heavy in unusual ways. Output is priced noticeably above input (4.0x), reflecting the typical chat-shape where the model spends more compute on generation than on reading the prompt. The context window is about 1M tokens, large enough to hold a substantial codebase, a long document, or an extended multi-turn conversation without truncation. OpenAI's pricing for this model is published through OpenRouter and refreshed every six hours; this page reads the live values, so the numbers here match what you would see if you called the OpenRouter models endpoint directly.

GPT-4.1 Nano is the right pick when cost is the main constraint. It fits well in: high-volume work: bulk classification, routing, extraction, and other batch jobs that fire thousands of times a day, routing layers in front of a more expensive model — use the cheap model to decide whether the expensive model is even needed, chat agents with short prompts and short answers, where the cost difference compounds quickly, and evaluation pipelines that need many invocations to compare prompt variants, where the cheaper model keeps the experiment budget realistic.

It is not the right pick for: workloads where a wrong answer is expensive — the budget tier is more likely to need a follow-up call or a human review, or the hardest reasoning tasks where the model has to work through a long chain of logic before answering. For those workloads, the right move is to pick a model from a different tier in the same provider lineup, or to use a different provider entirely.

When to use GPT-4.1 Nano

  • High-volume work: bulk classification, routing, extraction, batch jobs
  • Routing layers in front of a more expensive model
  • Short chat agents with short prompts and short answers
  • Evaluation pipelines that need many invocations to compare prompt variants

When NOT to use GPT-4.1 Nano

  • High-stakes reasoning where a wrong answer is expensive — the budget tier is tuned for throughput, not for the hardest analytical work. For those, step up to a mid or flagship tier, or move to a model with a reasoning capability flag.
  • Customer-facing chat where brand recognition matters — users notice when a response comes from a budget model, and the per-call cost saving is rarely worth the trust hit.

What is the context window for GPT-4.1 Nano?

The context window for GPT-4.1 Nano is 1,047,576 tokens, large enough to hold a substantial codebase or a long document without chunking. Anything longer has to be chunked or summarized before it fits.
